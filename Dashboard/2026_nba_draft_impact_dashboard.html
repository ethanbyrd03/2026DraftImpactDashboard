How the NBA impact score is calculated

This dashboard combines three analytical frameworks to project which 2026 draft prospects will have the highest NBA impact:

1. Raw BPM (Box Plus/Minus)
BPM estimates a player's impact per 100 possessions relative to average. Data sourced from Tankathon / BartTorvik for the 2025-26 season. OBPM measures offensive impact; DBPM measures defensive impact; BPM = OBPM + DBPM.

2. Age adjustment (Engelmann 2026)
Per the Royce Webb / Jeremias Engelmann methodology: each year younger than a 19-year-old baseline adds approximately +3 pts/100, +0.5 ast, +0.4 reb, +1% 3pt. For BPM, we apply a +0.9 BPM boost per year below age 19, and a −0.9 penalty per year above 19. Blocks show no aging effect and are unchanged.

3. Strength of schedule adjustment
The average SOS for drafted players is ~+7 SRS. Players who faced harder slates (e.g., Darryn Peterson at Kansas SRS +9.8) get a positive adjustment. Softer schedules (e.g., Bennett Stirtz at Iowa SRS +5.5) get a small negative adjustment. ~0.4 BPM per SRS unit from the +7 baseline.

4. NBA impact score (composite)
Weighted composite: Adj BPM (40%) + Adj OBPM (25%) + Adj DBPM (20%) + Age factor (10%) + Usage (5%). Scaled 0–100 relative to the class. Higher = more projected NBA impact. This is not a mock draft order — it is a statistical model and does not account for position scarcity, team fit, medical flags, or intangibles.

Key insight
Cameron Boozer led college basketball with a 17.1 BPM and is 18.9 years old — he receives the largest combined youth + high-BPM boost. Yaxel Lendeborg (23.7 years old) sees his impressive raw BPM heavily penalized. Aday Mara's elite DBPM of 8.4 makes him a standout despite modest offensive numbers.
